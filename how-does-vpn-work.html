The Fundamental Concept: What Is a VPN?

When I first started working as a network administrator at a Melbourne telecommunications company in 2008, one of my key responsibilities was helping corporate clients set up secure remote access to their company networks. This is where I first encountered VPN technology - Virtual Private Networks that created encrypted "tunnels" through the public internet.

At its core, a VPN is exactly what the name suggests: a private network connection that exists virtually rather than through dedicated physical cables. Instead of your data travelling directly from your device to a website or service, it first goes through an encrypted tunnel to a VPN server, which then forwards your request to the final destination.

Think of it like sending a letter through the post. Without a VPN, you're sending a postcard - anyone handling it along the way can read the message. With a VPN, you're sending that same message in a locked box that only you and the recipient can open. The postal workers (your ISP, network administrators, potential hackers) can see that a box is being sent, but they can't read what's inside.

VPN functionality relies on three fundamental technologies working together: encryption, tunnelling protocols, and authentication. Let me explain each component and why it matters for Australian users.

How VPN Encryption Protects Your Privacy

Encryption is the cornerstone of VPN security. When you connect to a VPN, specialised software on your device encrypts all outgoing data before it leaves your computer or phone. This encryption happens at such a low level that even your operating system can't read the data once it's encrypted.

Modern VPNs typically use military-grade encryption standards - specifically, AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) with 256-bit keys. To put this in perspective, AES-256 is the same encryption standard used by banks, governments, and military organisations worldwide. The computational power required to break this encryption through brute force would take billions of years with current technology.

From an Australian context, this encryption is particularly important given our mandatory data retention laws. Whilst your ISP must log your metadata under Australian law, they can only see that you're connected to a VPN server - not what you're doing through that connection. The contents of your communication remain encrypted and private.

One thing that surprised many of my clients is that encryption isn't actually "unbreakable" - it's just impractically difficult to break. AES-256 has 2^256 possible key combinations. This is why I emphasise choosing VPNs with strong encryption standards. Some budget providers cut corners by using outdated or weaker encryption algorithms to reduce server costs. These savings aren't worth the security compromise for Australian users subject to government surveillance capabilities.

What Happens to Your IP Address?

One of the most important functions of a VPN is IP address masking. Your IP address is like your home address on the internet - it identifies your location and can be used to track your online activities. When you're not using a VPN, every website you visit, every online service you use, and every email you send carries your real IP address.

Here's what happens when you connect to a VPN: Instead of websites seeing your actual IP address (assigned by your ISP and linked to your physical location in Australia), they see the IP address of the VPN server you're connected to. If you're connected to a VPN server in Sydney whilst physically located in Melbourne, websites think you're accessing them from Sydney. If you connect to a server in Singapore, they think you're in Singapore.

This IP masking serves multiple purposes for Australian users. It prevents websites from building profiles based on your location, helps you avoid the "Australia tax" where services charge higher prices to Australian IPs, and allows you to access geo-restricted content. When I'm researching VPN services, I always test whether the IP masking is complete - some poorly configured VPNs leak your real IP through DNS requests or WebRTC connections.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Misconception: Complete Anonymity

One of the biggest mistakes I see people make is believing that a VPN makes them completely anonymous online. It doesn't. Whilst a VPN significantly improves your privacy by encrypting your traffic and masking your IP address, you're not invisible.

Your VPN provider can still theoretically see your traffic (which is why choosing a trustworthy, no-logs provider is critical). Websites can still track you through cookies, browser fingerprinting, and account logins. If you log into Facebook through a VPN, Facebook still knows it's you regardless of your IP address.

VPNs provide privacy, not anonymity. For true anonymity, you'd need to combine a VPN with other tools like Tor, use dedicated anonymous browsers, avoid logging into personal accounts, and take numerous other precautions. For most Australian users, VPN-level privacy is sufficient and practical. Understanding these limitations helps you use VPNs effectively. The Journey of Your Data Through a VPN

Let me walk you through exactly what happens when you use a VPN to visit a website. I'll use a practical example: accessing ABC iView from overseas to watch Australian content.

Step 1: Initiating Connection - You open your VPN app and select a server in Sydney. Your VPN client software establishes a connection with that server using the VPN protocol (let's say WireGuard). During this handshake, your device and the server exchange encryption keys using asymmetric cryptography, ensuring only they can decrypt each other's messages.

Step 2: Encrypting Your Request - You open your browser and type in the ABC iView URL. Before that request leaves your device, your VPN software intercepts it and encrypts the entire data packet using the encryption key established in Step 1. The encrypted packet is then wrapped in another layer that contains routing information for the VPN server.

Step 3: Transmission to VPN Server - The encrypted packet travels through your ISP's network to the VPN server in Sydney. Your ISP can see that you're sending data to a VPN server (they know the VPN server's IP address), but they can't see what websites you're accessing or what content you're requesting. From their perspective, it's just encrypted gibberish.

Step 4: Decryption and Forwarding - The VPN server receives your encrypted request, decrypts it using its copy of the encryption key, and sees that you want to access ABC iView. It then makes that request on your behalf, using its own Sydney-based IP address. To ABC iView, this looks like a normal request from a Sydney user.

Step 5: Return Journey - ABC iView sends the streaming content back to the VPN server in Sydney. The server encrypts this content and sends it back through the encrypted tunnel to your device, wherever you actually are in the world. Your VPN client decrypts the content, and you can watch your Australian programmes.

This entire process happens in milliseconds. With a quality VPN optimised for Australian connections, you barely notice any speed difference. Poor VPNs, however, introduce significant latency due to inefficient routing, overloaded servers, or weak encryption implementations that require excessive processing power.

What VPNs Protect Against (And What They Don't)

After 15 years in IT security, I've encountered countless misconceptions about what VPNs can and cannot do. Let me be completely honest about the real protection VPNs offer for Australian users.

What VPNs Effectively Protect Against:

ISP Surveillance and Data Retention: Australian ISPs are required by law to retain metadata about your internet usage for two years. With a VPN, your ISP can only see that you're connected to a VPN server, not what specific websites you visit or services you use. This is one of the primary reasons I recommend VPNs for privacy-conscious Australians.

Public WiFi Attacks: Coffee shop WiFi, airport networks, and hotel internet are notoriously insecure. Without a VPN, anyone on the same network can potentially intercept your data using readily available hacking tools. A VPN encrypts all your traffic before it hits the WiFi network, making interception useless. As someone who works from Melbourne cafes regularly, I never connect to public WiFi without my VPN active.

Geographic Restrictions: Many streaming services, websites, and online services restrict access based on your location. VPNs allow you to bypass these restrictions by making it appear you're accessing from a different location. This works both ways - Australians travelling overseas can access Australian services, and Australians at home can access international content. However, streaming services are increasingly sophisticated at detecting VPNs, which is why I thoroughly test each VPN's streaming capabilities in my best VPN recommendations.

Price Discrimination: The "Australia tax" is real - many online services charge Australians significantly more for identical products or services. By appearing to access from locations with lower pricing, you can sometimes avoid these inflated costs for flights, hotels, software subscriptions, and digital services.

What VPNs Cannot Protect Against:

Malware and Viruses: A VPN encrypts your connection but doesn't scan for malicious software. If you download a virus through a VPN, you've downloaded a virus. You still need antivirus software, safe browsing habits, and regular software updates. Some VPN providers include malware blocking features, but these shouldn't be your primary protection.

Website-Level Tracking: If you log into Facebook, Google, or any other account-based service, that platform knows who you are regardless of your VPN. They track you through cookies, account logins, browser fingerprinting, and device identifiers. A VPN doesn't prevent this type of tracking - it only prevents others from seeing that you're accessing these sites.

Legal Consequences of Illegal Activities: Using a VPN doesn't give you licence to break the law. Whilst VPNs are completely legal in Australia (learn more in my article on VPN legality), using them for illegal activities remains illegal. Copyright infringement, harassment, fraud, and other crimes are still prosecutable regardless of VPN use. VPNs aren't "get out of jail free" cards.

Phishing and Social Engineering: If a scammer tricks you into revealing your password or banking details, a VPN can't help. These attacks target you directly rather than intercepting your traffic. VPNs protect data in transit, not data you willingly hand over.

üí° Mia's Practical Advice

The most common question I get from Australian clients is: "Will a VPN make me safe online?" My answer is always: "A VPN is essential for privacy, but it's just one component of online security."

Think of online security like home security. A VPN is like having good locks on your doors - absolutely essential, but not sufficient by itself. You also need a monitored alarm system (antivirus), secure windows (software updates), awareness of social engineering (not opening your door to strangers), and safe practices (not leaving valuables visible).

I use a VPN every single day, but I also use a password manager, enable two-factor authentication, keep my systems updated, use encrypted messaging apps, and maintain healthy scepticism about unsolicited communications. This layered approach provides comprehensive protection that no single tool can offer.

How to Choose VPN Technology That Works for You

Understanding how VPNs work helps you evaluate providers and choose technology suited to your needs. Based on my technical analysis and testing with Australian internet infrastructure, here are the key factors to consider:

Protocol Support: Ensure your VPN offers WireGuard or OpenVPN. These are the gold standards for 2026. IKEv2/IPsec is acceptable for mobile use. Avoid providers that only offer outdated protocols like L2TP or PPTP.

Encryption Standards: Look for AES-256 encryption. Some providers offer AES-128 as a "faster" option, but the security trade-off isn't worth the minimal speed gain for most Australian users.

Server Infrastructure: Check whether the provider owns their servers or rents them. Owned servers (sometimes called "bare metal") are preferable because the provider has complete control over security. Virtual servers can be fine if properly configured, but they introduce additional trust requirements.

Kill Switch Technology: A kill switch prevents your internet traffic from being exposed if the VPN connection drops unexpectedly. This is non-negotiable for privacy-focused users. Test that the kill switch actually works - some implementations are buggy.

DNS Leak Protection: DNS requests can expose your browsing history even when using a VPN if not properly configured. Quality VPNs route all DNS requests through their own secure DNS servers. I test for DNS leaks in all my VPN reviews.
