Do I Need a VPN? Honest Assessment for Your Situation

Mia Wexford, B.Sc. (IT Security), CISSP
Helping Australians make informed decisions about privacy technology

Not everyone needs a VPN, and I refuse to use scare tactics to convince you otherwise. After 15 years in cybersecurity, I believe in honest assessments based on actual needs and realistic threat models. Let's work through whether a VPN makes sense for your specific situation.

The Honest Answer: It Depends on Your Needs

The cybersecurity industry has a problem with fear-mongering. Many VPN companies and review sites use exaggerated threats and misleading claims to push their products. "Hackers are everywhere!" "Your ISP is spying on you!" "You're not safe without a VPN!" These scare tactics annoy me because they undermine informed decision-making.

Here's my honest professional opinion: most Australians would benefit from using a VPN, but not everyone needs one urgently, and the specific benefits vary based on your circumstances. Some people genuinely don't need VPN protection. Others absolutely should be using one. Many fall somewhere in between, where a VPN provides valuable benefits but isn't strictly essential.

Let me help you assess your own situation objectively. Understanding how VPNs work will help you evaluate whether the technology addresses your specific concerns. And knowing that VPNs are completely legal in Australia means you can make this decision purely based on benefits, not legal worries.

Quick Self-Assessment: Should You Use a VPN?

Answer These Questions Honestly

1. Do you frequently use public WiFi networks?

Coffee shops, airports, hotels, libraries, shopping centres - if you regularly connect to public networks, a VPN is highly recommended. Public WiFi is notoriously insecure, and without encryption, your data can be intercepted by anyone on the same network.

Answer YES: Strong VPN recommendation

2. Are you concerned about your ISP collecting and storing your browsing data?

Australian ISPs must retain metadata for two years under data retention laws. If this surveillance concerns you and you value privacy, a VPN significantly reduces what your ISP can meaningfully track about your activities.

Answer YES: VPN recommended

3. Do you travel internationally or need to access Australian services from overseas?

Accessing your Australian banking, streaming services, or other geo-restricted content whilst travelling requires a VPN. Many Australian expats also use VPNs to stay connected to local services.

Answer YES: VPN recommended for travel

4. Are you frustrated by the "Australia tax" on digital services?

Many international services charge Australians significantly more for identical products. Airlines, hotels, software subscriptions - price discrimination based on location is common. VPNs can sometimes help you access better pricing.

Answer YES: VPN potentially beneficial

5. Do you only browse major websites on your home network?

If you primarily use Facebook, Google, mainstream news sites, and similar services exclusively from your secure home WiFi, and you're not particularly concerned about ISP tracking, your need for a VPN is lower.

Answer YES: VPN less critical (but still potentially valuable)

6. Do you handle sensitive information online?

Journalists, lawyers, healthcare workers, financial professionals, or anyone dealing with confidential information should consider VPN protection as part of their security strategy.

Answer YES: Strong VPN recommendation

Interpreting Your Answers: If you answered YES to questions 1, 2, 3, or 6, you have clear reasons to use a VPN. If you answered YES to question 4, a VPN provides potential benefits. If you primarily answered YES only to question 5, your need for a VPN is lower, though you might still benefit from privacy protection.

What VPNs Actually Protect Against (Reality Check)

One reason I'm sometimes sceptical of VPN marketing is the exaggerated claims about what VPNs can do. Let me be brutally honest about what VPNs actually protect you from and what they don't.

Threat VPN Protection Explanation
ISP Tracking ‚úì STRONG VPNs effectively prevent ISPs from seeing which specific sites you visit and services you use.
Public WiFi Attacks ‚úì STRONG Encryption prevents anyone on the same network from intercepting your data.
Geo-Restrictions ‚úì EFFECTIVE VPNs reliably bypass geographic restrictions, though some streaming services detect and block VPNs.
Data Retention ‚úì PARTIAL Reduces utility of retained metadata but doesn't eliminate collection entirely.
Advertiser Tracking ‚óê LIMITED Helps with IP-based tracking but doesn't prevent cookie-based or account-based tracking.
Viruses/Malware ‚úó NONE VPNs don't scan for or protect against malicious software. You still need antivirus.
Phishing/Social Engineering ‚úó NONE VPNs can't protect you from being tricked into revealing information.
Website-Level Tracking ‚úó MINIMAL If you log into accounts, those services still know who you are regardless of VPN use.

This realistic assessment helps you understand what you're actually getting with a VPN. They're powerful privacy tools for specific threats but not comprehensive security solutions for all online dangers.

‚ö†Ô∏è Mia's Reality Check

I've consulted with clients who believed VPNs would make them "completely anonymous" or "totally secure" online. These beliefs are dangerous because they create false confidence.

VPNs are one layer of security. You also need strong passwords (use a password manager), two-factor authentication, antivirus software, regular updates, and most importantly, critical thinking about online threats.

If a VPN provider claims their service makes you "completely anonymous" or "100% secure," that's marketing nonsense. Run away and choose a provider that makes honest, realistic claims about their capabilities.

Value Assessment: What Are You Getting?

For Daily Coffee Shop Workers ($4.50/month): If you work from cafes even once or twice per week, the protection from public WiFi attacks alone justifies the cost. A single data breach or identity theft incident would cost you far more in time and money to resolve.

For Privacy-Conscious Home Users ($4.50/month): Compare this to other privacy expenses. You probably pay $10+ monthly for streaming services. Is privacy worth less than entertainment? For many Australians subject to mandatory data retention, the privacy benefit is worth the modest cost.

For Frequent International Travellers ($4.50/month): The value is obvious. Secure access to Australian services whilst overseas, protection on foreign hotel networks, and avoiding geographic restrictions make this a trivial expense compared to travel costs.

For Casual Home Users ($4.50/month): This is where honest assessment matters. If you're on a tight budget, rarely leave your home network, and aren't particularly concerned about ISP tracking, that $4.50 monthly might be better spent elsewhere. VPN benefits exist but may not justify the cost for your specific situation.

When clients tell me they can't afford a VPN, I ask about their other subscriptions. Often, people pay $50+ monthly for streaming services they barely use whilst claiming they can't spare $5 for privacy protection.

If you genuinely have a tight budget, prioritise: First, secure your accounts with strong passwords and 2FA (free). Second, keep your software updated (free). Third, use reputable antivirus (some free options exist). If you can then afford $5 monthly, add VPN protection.

What I don't recommend: free VPNs. If you're not paying for the product, you are the product. Free VPNs often collect and sell your data, inject ads, or provide inadequate security. Paying a modest amount for quality VPN service is worth it if you're going to use one at all.

Calculating Your Personal Value

Here's a practical framework for assessing whether a VPN is worth the cost for you specifically:

Step 1: List your specific concerns (ISP tracking, public WiFi, geo-restrictions, etc.)

Step 2: Identify which concerns VPNs actually address (use my table above)

Step 3: Estimate how frequently you encounter these situations

Step 4: Compare the ~$5 monthly cost to the value of addressing those concerns

For most Australians in scenarios where VPNs provide clear benefits, $5 monthly is excellent value. For those where benefits are marginal, the cost might not be justified. Only you can make this determination based on your circumstances and values.

Alternatives to VPNs: Other Privacy Tools

Honest cybersecurity advice acknowledges that VPNs aren't the only privacy tools available. Depending on your specific concerns, other approaches might be more appropriate or should be used in combination with VPNs.

For ISP Tracking Concerns

Encrypted DNS (DNS-over-HTTPS): Prevents ISPs from seeing your DNS queries. Doesn't provide the same comprehensive protection as a VPN, but it's free and addresses one specific tracking vector. Firefox and Chrome both support encrypted DNS natively.

For Advertiser Tracking Concerns

Privacy-Focused Browsers: Brave, Firefox with privacy extensions, or DuckDuckGo browser block trackers, cookies, and fingerprinting. These address advertiser tracking more effectively than VPNs do. Use these in addition to a VPN for comprehensive protection.

For Maximum Anonymity Needs

Tor Browser: For journalists, activists, or whistleblowers needing strong anonymity, Tor provides better protection than standard VPNs. It's slower and more complex to use properly, but for high-threat scenarios, it's the better tool. Some security experts recommend using VPNs and Tor together.

For Specific Security Threats

Targeted Security Tools: Antivirus for malware, password managers for credential security, 2FA for account protection, encrypted messaging for communication security. These address threats that VPNs don't cover.

The point is that VPNs are powerful tools for specific privacy needs, but they're part of a broader security toolkit rather than standalone solutions.
